Understanding the hidden environmental cost of artificial intelligence

Why does AI consume water?

Training and running AI models requires massive computing power in large data centers. These servers generate significant heat, which must be cooled to avoid damage. One of the most common cooling methods uses water: chilled water circulates through cooling systems, absorbing heat and then evaporating or being discharged.

The bigger the AI model, the more servers are required, and the more cooling ‚Äî and therefore water ‚Äî is needed.

How do we measure this consumption?

Water consumption in AI data centers is typically measured using a metric called Water Usage Effectiveness (WUE).

  • Liters per kWh: How many liters of water are consumed per kilowatt-hour of electricity used.
  • Total liters per training run: Estimates for large models can reach millions of liters for a single training cycle.
  • Geographic impact: Water use varies greatly depending on whether a data center is located in a water-scarce region.

What is the impact?

The environmental impact of AI‚Äôs water use is complex. On one hand, advanced AI research drives innovation that could help manage water resources and fight climate change. On the other hand, data centers can put stress on local water supplies, particularly in drought-prone areas.

If unchecked, the increasing demand for AI could significantly contribute to water scarcity, competing with agriculture and communities.

Transparency from tech companies and the adoption of sustainable cooling methods ‚Äî such as air cooling, recycled water, or renewable energy integration ‚Äî are essential to minimize this impact.
